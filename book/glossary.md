# Glossary

In the definitions in this glossary, terms that refer to concepts that are defined elsewhere in the glossary are printed in *italic*.

## General terms from psychology

**Adoption**

Adoption refers to the initial decision by key stakeholders or implementers to begin using a new program, policy, or practice. It is a critical step in the implementation process, marking the point at which an organization or individual commits to trying or integrating the intervention into their existing systems or routines.

**Behavior**

Behavior is often defined as muscle activity. In Intervention Mapping, behavior is defined a bit more broadly and can include decisions people take. Behaviors are always processes that take place over time.

**Construct**

In psychology, a construct is usually a hypothesized psychological variable or process. Construct definitions describe which aspects of human psychology comprise that construct.

**Determinant**

In behavior change science, a determinant, also called behavioral determinant or psycho-social determinant, is a modifiable psychological construct that is hypothesized to cause behavior or contribute to causing behavior by at least one theory.

**Effect evaluation**

Effect evaluation is the process of assessing whether a program has successfully met its intended goals and outcomes. In the context of Intervention Mapping, this means evaluating the specific changes the intervention aimed to achieve, such as improvements in health outcomes, behaviors, quality of life, environmental conditions, and changes in key determinants such as knowledge, attitudes, skills, or self-efficacy.

**Fidelity**

Fidelity refers to the extent to which a program is implemented as it was originally designed, with all of its intended behavior change methods and practical applications applied correctly and consistently.

**Implementation**

Implementation refers to the actual use of a program, intervention, or innovation by those who have adopted it. It goes beyond the initial decision to adopt and involves putting the program into practice consistently and with fidelity over a period long enough to assess its effectiveness and determine whether it meets the perceived needs of the target population.

**Logic model**

A logic model is a diagram that visualizes theoretical, empirically derived, or hypothetical relationships between concepts.

**Maintenance**

Maintenance means that the program is not just temporarily used, but continues to be delivered consistently and effectively as part of an organization’s standard operations. It reflects a long-term commitment by the organization or system to continue supporting the intervention both structurally and behaviorally.

**Pilot-testing**

Pilot testing involves implementing the entire program on a small scale with both participants and implementers. This allows planners to identify and resolve any practical or logistical issues, assess how the program functions in a real-world setting, and make adjustments before the full-scale rollout.

**Pre-testing**

Pretesting is the process of evaluating specific components of an intervention, such as messages, visuals, or materials, with members of the target audience before they are finalized. The goal is to assess aspects like clarity, relevance, and cultural appropriateness to ensure the materials are well understood and effective.

**Process evaluation**

Process evaluation examines how the program was implemented, and is focusing on whether the planned activities and strategies were carried out as intended. It assesses aspects such as fidelity (whether the program was delivered according to plan), the reach (how many participants were involved), and the quality of delivery. This type of evaluation helps identify strengths and weaknesses in the program’s execution and provides insights for improvement.

**Socio-ecological model**

The Socio-Ecological Model explains that human behavior is influenced by multiple levels of factors, not just individual choices. It emphasizes that behavior is shaped through the interaction between personal factors and the broader social and environmental context. This model includes several interconnected levels: the individual level, the interpersonal level, the organizational level, the community level, and the policy level.

**Theory**

A theory is an explanation of a bit of the world. Theories in the social sciences often hypothesize and define one or more psychological constructs and then describe how they relate to each other.

## Intervention Mapping terms

**ABCD**

An Acyclic Behavior Change Diagram is to step 3 of IM what a Matrix of Change Objectives is to step 2: it succinctly illustrates structural-causal chains in an intervention logic model.

**At-risk Group**

At-risk group refers to the population that is directly experiencing or is vulnerable to the health problem. For clarification on how this differs from the target group, see ‘’Target Group’’.

**Change Objective**

Change Objectives are *subdeterminants* that have been formulated according to a specific set of guidelines. They are representations of the corresponding subdeterminant in a measurable form.

**Environmental Agent**

Environmental agents, sometimes called environmental actors, are people or groups of people that have agency over one or more environmental conditions.

**Environmental Condition**

An environmental condition is an aspect of the environment of the target population, of an environmental agent, or of an implementer.

**Environmental Level**

An environmental level is the level at which an environmental agent exists. These levels determine which methods are available for targeting those agents.

**Parameters for effectiveness**

Parameters for effectiveness, also known as parameters for use, are the specific conditions that must be met for a theory-based behavior change method to work as intended.

**Performance Objective**

A performance objective is a sub-behavior. The concept is used to get more fine-grained insights into the determinants and environmental conditions that together predict a behavior.

**Sub-determinant**

A sub-determinant is an *aspect* that has been categorized as a specific psychological construct, that is, a determinant. These are important in behavior change science and practice because behavior change interventions can only target concrete and specific content.

**Target Group**

The target group (or target population) is the group at whom the intervention is directed to create change. While the target group and the at-risk group may sometimes overlap, they are not always the same. For instance, when the health issue involves young children, the at-risk group is the children themselves, but the target group may be their parents, who have the influence to change behaviors or health outcomes. In cases of malnutrition among children under the age of five, the at-risk group is the children, while the target group for the intervention may be the mothers, as they have a central role in feeding and caregiving.

## Non-IM behavior change terms

**Aspect of the human psyche**

An aspect of the human psyche is a raw expression of somebody's psyche, for example "I really hate broccoli!" or "At that party, everybody was drinking cocktails."

**COMPLECS**

COMPLECS is a recursive acronym for COMPLECS Organises Multiple Players & Linked Environments using Connected Specifications.

**CIBER plot**

A Confidence Interval-Based Estimation of Relevance plot is a visualization to help select sub-determinants to intervene on.

**MAP**

A Map of Aspects of the Psyche, MAP, shows all psychological aspects that may cause a target behavior.
